The question was never whether to work, but how to work, what to work on, and for whom. Today, for the first time in human history, that question is becoming genuinely possible to ask.

> The Axial Age made it possible for a tiny minority to ask: what is the good life beyond survival?
> The age we are entering may make it necessary for everyone to ask this question.
>
> This is, I believe, the most important reason to think seriously about AI and Universal Basic Income — not as a policy debate about labor economics, but as a civilizational question about what humans are for.

> We should be honest about what Universal Basic Income is not. It is not a magical solution. It is not a panacea that will automatically produce human flourishing. It is not a simple policy fix that will resolve the civilizational transition we are entering.

# The Question That Should Not Be Possible

In a conversation with Professor Jaeseok Huh at Soongsil University in November 2025, a thought struck me with unusual force. It was not a new thought in any literal sense — Universal Basic Income has been discussed for centuries. But the *force* of the thought, the sense that something previously theoretical was becoming structurally inevitable, hit me as something genuinely new.

This essay is an attempt to think through what I have come to believe is one of the most important questions our generation will face: *given the trajectory of Artificial Intelligence (AI), what becomes of work, meaning, and human flourishing in a world where human labor may no longer be economically necessary?* And more pointedly: *what happens to the survival imperative that has structured nearly all of human civilization?*

I want to be honest at the outset that I am not certain Universal Basic Income is the right answer. What I am certain of is that the question we have been asking — *which jobs will be replaced and which will survive?* — is the wrong question. It assumes the old frame. It assumes work as we have known it must persist. The deeper question is whether the historical accident of the survival-work coupling, which has persisted even past the Axial Age, is finally coming to its end — and what that means for what it means to be human.

# The Long Arc — From Survival to Meaning to What?

For nearly all of human existence, our ancestors lived as hunter-gatherers. The cognitive landscape was dominated by a single imperative: survive! Survive harsh nature, savage beasts, starvation, disease, the violence of other humans. There was no meaningful distinction between &ldquo;work&rdquo; and &ldquo;life&rdquo; because every waking moment was, in some sense, oriented toward continued existence.

Then something remarkable happened. Around the middle of the first millennium BCE, in a development that the philosopher Karl Jaspers in 1949 termed the [Axial Age](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axial_Age){:target="_blank"}, human civilizations across Persia, India, China, the [Levant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Levant){:target="_blank"}, and the [Greco-Roman world](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greco-Roman_world){:target="_blank"} underwent a near-simultaneous awakening. Buddha, Confucius, [Lao-tzu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laozi){:target="_blank"}, the Hebrew prophets, Socrates, Plato, Aristotle, and countless others began asking questions that had no direct survival utility: What is the good life? What is justice? What is the nature of reality? What lies beyond death?

<span class="emph">This is one of the most extraordinary facts about human history: at multiple points around the globe, more or less simultaneously, humans began pursuing meaning, beauty, virtue, and transcendence as ends in themselves. Artists pursued beauty. Philosophers pursued truth. Mystics pursued union with the divine. Most people, of course, continued to live within the survival imperative — but a thin layer of human activity finally escaped it.</span>

Yet here is the crucial observation: *for the vast majority of human beings, the survival imperative remained dominant even past the Axial Age*. The contemplative life, the artistic life, the philosophical life — these remained the privileges of small elites, often supported by the labor of others or by inherited resources. The fundamental human condition, for nearly everyone, remained: *work to live*.

The Industrial Revolution, the invention of synthetic fertilizers, the serendipitous discovery of penicillin — these dramatically increased human productivity and reduced the risk of starvation and disease. But they did not break the survival-work coupling. They reorganized it. The peasant became the factory worker. The factory worker became the office worker. The office worker now sits at a desk from 9 to 5, Monday to Friday, doing work that often bears no obvious relationship to direct human survival but which remains, structurally, *what one must do to live*.

# The Question I Could Never Stop Asking

For reasons I have only partially understood, I have always struggled to take this arrangement for granted. Where most people around me accepted &ldquo;you must work&rdquo; as a kind of cosmic given, I kept asking: *why?*

I now understand why I could never find a satisfying answer. **There is no satisfying answer.** There is no inevitable cosmic necessity that humans must work. The only genuine necessity is that we must survive — and *given a particular economic and technological arrangement*, work has been the means of survival. But this is contingent, not necessary. The coupling of survival and work is a historical fact, not a metaphysical truth.

This distinction matters enormously, because if the coupling is contingent on a particular technological and economic arrangement, then changes in that arrangement can decouple them. And we are now living through exactly such a change.

# The AI Inflection Point

The technological story is now familiar to most readers, but its philosophical implications are only beginning to be absorbed. The introduction of [AlexNet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/AlexNet){:target="_blank"} in 2012 marked the practical viability of deep learning. The 2017 paper "[Attention is All You Need](https://arxiv.org/abs/1706.03762){:target="_blank"}" introduced the Transformer architecture that would become the foundation of modern large language models (LLMs). The release of ChatGPT to the public in November 2022 brought generative AI into mainstream consciousness. Subsequent developments in LLMs, multimodal models, and what has come to be called "agentic AI" have proceeded at a pace that has surprised even those of us who have spent decades in the field.

What is genuinely new about this moment is not just that AI can do impressive things. What is new is which kinds of human work AI is now capable of performing. The conventional wisdom of even a decade ago held that automation would replace manual labor while preserving cognitive work. The reverse appears to be happening. Software engineers — among the most cognitively demanding professions — are being significantly augmented and in some cases replaced. Writers, analysts, lawyers, doctors, designers, researchers — work that was supposed to require irreducibly human intelligence — turns out to be substantially automatable. Meanwhile, robotics — what is increasingly called "physical AI" — is finally beginning to address the manual tasks that resisted automation through the 2010s.

We have not arrived at the moment when most humans cannot find work. But the trajectory is unmistakable. And here is where I believe most contemporary discussion goes wrong.

# The Wrong Frame — Which Jobs Will Survive?

Most analysis of AI's labor implications takes the form of asking: *which jobs will be replaced, and which jobs will be safe?* This is the dominant framing in business journalism, in policy discussion, in academic research. It is the frame within which workers are advised to &ldquo;reskill&rdquo; and within which governments design retraining programs.

I believe this frame is fundamentally backwards.

It is backwards because it implicitly assumes that the survival-work coupling must be preserved — that the question is merely how to redistribute humans across the new landscape of &ldquo;AI-resistant&rdquo; jobs. It treats the structural arrangement of "humans must work to live" as a fixed boundary condition rather than as the historically contingent feature it actually is.

The deeper question is not which jobs will survive AI. The deeper question is: *given that AI can perform a rapidly expanding range of economically valuable work, should we continue to organize human society around the imperative that everyone must perform such work to access basic resources?*

The honest answer, I think, is that this organization made sense when human labor was the primary input to economic productivity. It is becoming progressively less defensible as that ceases to be true.

This is not an argument that humans should stop working. It is an argument that the *coupling* of "having access to food, shelter, healthcare, and dignity" with "selling one's labor in the market" — a coupling that has persisted for the entire span of agricultural and industrial human history — may finally be ready to be broken.

# The New Paradigm — Human-Machine Collaboration

There is another reason the &ldquo;which jobs will survive?&rdquo; frame is inadequate. It assumes a clean replacement dynamic — humans out, machines in. But what we are actually witnessing is much more complex: the emergence of new forms of human-machine collaboration that look like neither pure human work nor pure automation.

<span class="emph">When a software engineer works with an AI coding assistant, they are not being replaced; they are being transformed into something new</span> — a kind of *director* of computational creativity, whose value lies in judgment, intent, taste, and integration rather than in raw production of code. When a researcher works with AI to analyze literature, they are not being replaced; they are operating at a level of synthesis that was previously impossible. When a doctor works with AI diagnostic tools, the relationship is not replacement but augmentation toward outcomes neither could achieve alone.

The question is whether this collaborative paradigm scales. I believe it does, but unevenly. Some kinds of human contribution become *more* valuable in collaboration with AI — judgment, creativity, ethical reasoning, contextual wisdom, deep cross-domain integration. Other kinds become rapidly less valuable. The economic consequences of this asymmetry will be profound, and they will not be evenly distributed.

This is precisely why the question of universal basic income (UBI) becomes urgent. If AI dramatically increases overall productivity but concentrates the gains among those who own the AI infrastructure and those whose skills happen to complement it, then we face not utopia but a new and more severe form of inequality. The question is whether we will design institutions to distribute the productivity gains broadly — through some mechanism such as UBI — or whether we will allow them to concentrate to the point of social collapse.

# What I Want to Be Honest About

Before going further, I want to be transparent about what I do not know.

I do not know whether Universal Basic Income (UBI), as commonly proposed, would work as intended. The empirical evidence from pilot programs in Finland, Stockton California, Kenya, and elsewhere is genuinely mixed. Some studies suggest improvements in mental health and well-being without significant reductions in employment. Others suggest more modest effects. None to my knowledge demonstrate the transformative liberation that UBI proponents sometimes promise.

I do not know whether humans, freed from the survival imperative, would flourish or flounder. There is genuine reason for concern. As I argued in [{{ why_we_live.title }}]({{ why_we_live.url }}){:target="_blank"}, most people have not done the contemplative work necessary to handle existence without externally imposed structure. <span class="emph">Work, for all its discontents, has provided structure, identity, social belonging, and a sense of purpose for most people throughout history. Removing the necessity of work without simultaneously developing new structures of meaning could produce widespread existential disorientation rather than flourishing.</span>

I do not know how to navigate the political economy. UBI requires resources. Those resources must come from somewhere — most likely from taxation of the AI-driven productivity gains. But the entities likely to capture those gains are precisely those most able to resist taxation. The transition from "humans must work to live" to "humans are guaranteed basic resources regardless of work" is not merely a policy change — it is a fundamental restructuring of political and economic power, and history suggests such restructurings are rarely achieved through reasoned argument alone.

I do not know whether the timeline is what its most enthusiastic proponents suggest. AI capabilities are advancing rapidly, but the gap between technical capability and economic deployment is often longer than expected. The labor market disruptions may unfold over decades rather than years, allowing for more gradual adaptation than catastrophic-scenario thinking suggests.

These uncertainties are real and I do not want to wave them away. But I do not believe they undermine the central observation: <span class="emph">the survival-work coupling is becoming contingent rather than necessary, and we will need to think more clearly than ever before about what human existence is for once survival ceases to organize it.</span>

# The Existential Dimension

This brings us to what I believe is the deepest layer of the question — the layer that the policy discussions almost entirely miss.

If AI does eventually decouple survival from work for a significant portion of humanity, we will face an existential crisis that human civilization has never faced at scale. The questions that occupied a thin layer of contemplatives, philosophers, mystics, and artists throughout history will suddenly become questions that ordinary people must confront in their daily lives:

- What am I for, if not for work?
- What gives my life meaning, if not the necessity of providing for myself and my family?
- What do I do with my hours, if they are no longer claimed by economic necessity?
- Who am I, if not what I do for a living?

For what it is worth, I have grappled with these questions more
personally than this essay might suggest. My explorations —
documented in "[{{ why_we_live.title }}]({{ why_we_live.url }}){:target="_blank"}" and its
sequel "[{{ back_to_human.title }}]({{ back_to_human.url }}){:target="_blank"}"
— led me to a conclusion that is less an answer than a reframing:
<span class="emph">these questions may be wrong questions to ask in the first place</span>.
Not because they are unimportant, but because they assume a self
that exists separately from the living of a life — waiting to be
assigned meaning from outside. What I found, through a long process
I would hesitate to call anything other than genuine philosophical struggle,
which, though, is actually something beyond any philosophical conclusion (in most people's perspectives), is that meaning is not discovered but created — not
received but chosen. I believe this recognition — that we are the
authors of our own purpose, that the deepest human capacity is the
ability to choose what we honor in a universe that honors nothing —
truly touches something fundamental in the human soul.

This is not
merely my personal orientation. It is, I believe, the actual ground
we will need to stand on when the old structures fall away. The
deepest preparation for a post-survival world is not economic or
institutional but <span class="emph">contemplative</span>. It requires developing,
at scale, what most people have never needed to develop — the
capacity to be the author of one's own meaning.

These are precisely the questions that 19th and early 20th century existentialist and phenomenological philosophers — [Edmund Husserl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edmund_Husserl){:target="_blank"}, [Søren Kierkegaard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/S%C3%B8ren_Kierkegaard){:target="_blank"}, [Friedrich Nietzsche](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friedrich_Nietzsche){:target="_blank"}, [Martin Heidegger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Martin_Heidegger){:target="_blank"}, [Jean-Paul Sartre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean-Paul_Sartre){:target="_blank"}, [Albert Camus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Albert_Camus){:target="_blank"}, [Maurice Merleau-Ponty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maurice_Merleau-Ponty){:target="_blank"} — recognized as the fundamental questions facing modern humanity. Their work was prescient in a way that even they did not fully grasp. They were writing for a future in which the inherited structures of meaning had collapsed but the survival imperative still organized daily life. They were writing for *us* — for a future in which even the survival imperative may finally release its grip.

The Axial Age made it possible for a tiny minority to ask: <span class="emph">what is the good life beyond survival?</span>
<span style="color: red; font-style: italic;">The age we are entering may make it necessary for everyone to ask this question.</span>

<span class="emph">This is, I believe, the most important reason to think seriously about AI and Universal Basic Income — not as a policy debate about labor economics, but as a civilizational question about what humans are for.</span>

# Toward a Contemplative Response

We should be honest about what Universal Basic Income is not. It is not a magical solution. It is not a panacea that will automatically produce human flourishing. It is not a simple policy fix that will resolve the civilizational transition we are entering. To present it as such would be to indulge in the same technocratic fantasy that has repeatedly failed us — the illusion that complex human problems have simple systemic answers.

If anything, UBI might be one small component of what we will need. Perhaps even a necessary component. But by itself, it cannot address the deeper crisis we face when the survival imperative releases its organizing grip on human existence.

## What We Actually Need

The survival-work decoupling demands something far more profound than economic policy. It demands that we engage seriously with the fundamental questions of human existence — questions that have occupied the humanities and social sciences for millennia but which we have treated as peripheral luxuries, separate from "real" work in economics and governance.

We need to explore deeply — not superficially, not as academic exercises, but as urgent practical necessities — the insights from philosophy, cognitive science, psychology, anthropology, sociology, the contemplative traditions, and yes, even spiritual domains that speak to how humans create meaning, sustain purpose, and flourish in the absence of external compulsion. These are not soft disciplines. They are the disciplines that address the hardest question: <span class="emph">what is a human life for?</span>

This exploration cannot happen in isolation. It must involve genuine collaboration among many constituencies; those who understand human dimensions (philosophers, psychologists, cognitive scientists, contemplatives), those who understand technology (AI researchers, engineers who grasp the deep inner workings of prevailing algorithms and architectures), those who understand policy and governance (lawmakers, regulators, government officials), those who understand economics and markets, those who lead in the private sector, those who organize in civil society, and many others. Not the performative "stakeholder engagement" that has become standard in policy-making, but genuine contemplative work: sustained inquiry, honest reckoning with complexity, willingness to sit with uncertainty, and the intellectual humility to recognize that we are entering genuinely new territory.

The solutions, if they exist, will emerge from this synthesis. They will require new forms of governance, new regulatory frameworks, new institutional arrangements, new educational paradigms, new cultural campaigns to shift how we understand work and worth, new mechanisms for meaning-making at scale. Universal Basic Income might be one element.
But it is this multidimensional work — contemplative, technical, institutional, economic, done seriously, done together — that will determine whether that element functions as part of a coherent response or as an expensive distraction.

## Why the Solutions in Those Essays Matter

For what it is worth, I have grappled with these questions more personally than this essay might suggest. My explorations — documented in "[{{ why_we_live.title }}]({{ why_we_live.url }}){:target="_blank"}" and its sequel "[{{ back_to_human.title }}]({{ back_to_human.url }}){:target="_blank"}" — are not merely intellectual exercises or philosophical speculation.

I believe the territory explored in those essays — the recognition that meaning is created rather than discovered, that we are the authors of our own purpose, that the deepest human capacity is the ability to choose what we honor in a universe that honors nothing — truly touches something fundamental in the human soul. Not as abstract philosophy, but as lived reality. Not as theory, but as the actual ground on which we will need to stand when the old structures fall away.

These are, I believe, the most practical solutions that will actually work. Not because they provide easy answers or simple frameworks, but because they address the actual territory we will need to navigate: the existential dimension of post-survival existence. The capacity to be fully human in the absence of external necessity. The ability to construct purpose consciously rather than inherit it from circumstances.

But — and this is crucial — these contemplative insights must be coupled with pragmatic action across every dimension of society. We need new laws that protect human agency while enabling economic transition. We need regulatory frameworks that prevent the concentration of AI-driven wealth while funding the social infrastructure for meaningful existence. We need educational systems that cultivate the capacity for autonomous meaning-making. We need cultural movements that shift how we understand value and worth. We need economic policies, yes, but also philosophical education, contemplative practice, institutional innovation, and political courage.

The solution is not one thing. It is everything, done together, done seriously, done with full awareness of what is at stake.

## The Question Remains

The hunter-gatherer worked to survive. The Axial Age contemplative worked partly to survive and partly to seek meaning. The industrial worker worked to survive within new institutions. <span class="emph">We may be entering an age in which the question is no longer how to survive, but how to live.</span>

I do not claim to have answered this question. <span class="emph">I claim only to have recognized its urgency and begun the contemplative work it demands. The Axial Age made it possible for a tiny minority to ask: what is the good life beyond survival? The age of AI may make it necessary for everyone to ask this question.</span>

This is not a transition we can navigate through policy papers and committee meetings alone. It will require the deepest resources of human wisdom — philosophical, psychological, spiritual, and practical — brought to bear on the most consequential question our generation will face.

We had better begin in earnest!
